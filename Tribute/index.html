Tribute To Indian Farmers

Indian farmers who play a vital role in ensuring food security and sustaining the nation's economy.

Indian agriculture is largely dependent on the hard work of farmers, who form the backbone of the country's agrarian economy. They work tirelessly to cultivate crops and provide essential food items to the nation's population. Despite facing several challenges such as unpredictable weather conditions, rising input costs, and low prices for their produce, Indian farmers have continued to persevere and work towards ensuring food security.


Some Information about our Indian Farmers

  • Agriculture is the primary occupation for about 58% of India's population. According to the World Bank, agriculture accounts for approximately 17% of India's GDP.
  • The Green Revolution of the 1960s and 70s, which aimed to increase agricultural productivity in the country, was a significant milestone in Indian agriculture. Farmers were able to produce more food than ever before, and the country's food security improved considerably. Since then, farmers have continued to innovate and adopt new technologies to improve their yields and efficiency.
  • The recent agricultural reforms have brought farmers into the national spotlight, with many protesting against the new laws. The protests highlight the crucial role that farmers play in ensuring food security and the need for policies that support and protect their interests.
  • Indian farmers grow a wide range of crops, including rice, wheat, maize, pulses, oilseeds, cotton, sugarcane, fruits, and vegetables. India is the world's second-largest producer of wheat and rice, and the largest producer of pulses.
  • Indian farmers face several challenges, including small land holdings, lack of access to credit, water scarcity, inadequate irrigation facilities, and post-harvest losses. The high cost of inputs, such as seeds, fertilizers, and pesticides, also poses a significant challenge.
  • The Indian government has implemented several policies and programs to support farmers, including providing subsidized inputs, crop insurance, and minimum support prices for select crops. However, these policies have faced criticism for being insufficient and not reaching all farmers.
  • The recent protests by farmers in India are largely against the new farm laws passed by the government in 2020. The laws aim to reform the agriculture sector by allowing farmers to sell their produce outside government-regulated mandis and contract farming. However, farmers fear that the laws will leave them vulnerable to exploitation by large corporations and undermine their existing support systems.
  • In recent years, some farmers in India have also adopted organic farming practices and diversified into non-farming activities, such as agro-tourism and food processing. These initiatives have the potential to increase farmers' income and reduce their dependence on traditional agriculture.
